# BDS OS — what it is and why it exists

## What it is
A P&L-driven operating-maturity and capital-allocation system. Software that helps a CEO and their leadership team turn "running a business" from instinct and meeting-fatigue into a **measurable, prioritized, weekly compass**.

## The problem it solves
CEOs face dozens of competing demands — sales, ops, hiring, finance, governance — and finite leadership attention. Most dashboards tell you *what happened* (revenue, churn, burn). They don't tell you **what to actually work on this quarter to make the business better**. Static maturity frameworks (EOS, Rockefeller Habits, McKinsey checklists) ignore the company's stage, financial pressure, or which practices actually move the needle *now*.

## How it works
Five layers, each grounded in real data:

1. **Practice ontology** — 82 management practices across 8 areas, each with a 5-level maturity rubric. "Level 3 in cash forecasting" means something specific and verifiable.
2. **OPI (Operational Priority Index)** — scores every practice on importance × competency gap × P&L impact × speed-to-impact × dependency × risk, weighted by the company's **lifecycle stage** (startup → growth → scale → mature). The same competency gap matters differently at different stages.
3. **WIP-limited focus portfolio** — each quarter, the system selects the small set of practices to actively work on. Caps ambition to what's actually doable.
4. **Execution + evidence loop** — initiatives, evidence uploads, AI grading against the rubric, formal score-change requests, senior approval. Maturity scores only go up when there's *documented* change, not self-reported.
5. **Governance dashboards** — three distinct views: executive (this-week focus + risk alerts), board (long-term maturity trajectory + operating debt), functional (per-leader owned practices + pending evidence queue).

## Who it's for
- **Primary**: new or first-time CEOs stepping into leadership and looking for a Monday-morning compass.
- **Secondary**: their leadership team (CFO, COO, functional heads) who score their own areas and own initiatives in their domain.
- **Tertiary**: the board — a quarterly snapshot of how the business is maturing, harder to fake than self-reported KPIs.

## How it helps
1. **Replaces gut-feel prioritization with a weighted, lifecycle-aware ranking**, so leadership attention and capital go to practices that actually move *this* company's P&L *now*.
2. **Enforces WIP discipline** — caps how many practices can be active per quarter, so leadership doesn't dilute focus across 30 things and finish 0.
3. **Makes maturity provable** — every level-up requires evidence graded against the rubric, then a formal approval. Cuts off self-reported scoring inflation.
4. **Surfaces operating debt** — practices below their risk floor, expired evidence, areas degrading quietly. Like technical debt, but for the business.
5. **Makes decisions and approvals visible** — CEOs see their pending approval queue; leaders see their owned initiatives. No more "where did this stall?"

## The value

| Stakeholder | Value |
|---|---|
| **CEO** | Clarity on what matters this week. Less reactive firefighting, more compounding investment in capability. |
| **Leadership team** | A shared model of what "good" looks like in their function, and a queue of well-scoped initiatives instead of vague directives. |
| **Board** | A maturity scoreboard grounded in evidence + AI rubric grading + senior approval — harder to fake than self-reported KPIs, and visible quarter-over-quarter. |
| **Investors / acquirers** | An operational due-diligence artifact: where the business is mature, where it's exposed, how fast it's improving. |

## The first-use scenario
A new CEO opens bds-OS on a Monday morning. They see:
- The 3 Phase-1 practices the system says matter most this week
- Risk-floor breaches that need attention
- The pending-approval queue waiting on them
- Their team's progress on the active focus portfolio

They spend 15 minutes there instead of 2 hours scrolling Slack and trying to remember what their COO mentioned last Friday. Then they go run the company — but with a compass, not a wishlist.
